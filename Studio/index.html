Technical Studies

Tech Studies has provided a wonderful practical grounding and has played a part in most of the ways I've been trying to work this year. I've also learned loads about putting things online. I knew nothing about opensource, code, languages (HTML/CSS/Javascript) and now know enough to have built this website! I enjoy how everything is so entangled, and the website gives me a platform to collate all of my work and practices in one place. I did have to outsource some help (in the form of Hacksmiths) in order to get the website working, but the module allowed me to see the potential of web. The explorations from the web workshop can be viewed here. I gained a lot of appreciation for how taking something further by yourself can be so beneficial. In the past I've gone to Risograph printing workshops and bookbinding workshops in order to further my understanding of print and paper arts- and I'm so grateful for a grounding in something digital. I'd like to find something like this that is web-related and maybe this manifests in a short course somewhere or teaming up with a Hacksmiths team member on one of my projects in the future. Building the website is just the first step...

It's funny how different materials behave in different ways. We were told that the alginate would decomopse/lose its form within a week or so, wheras the Jesmonite (a plaster/resin mix) would last a very long time. Experimenting With Colour

Experimenting with form, colour and material produced a range of interesting outcomes. Using moulds and casts allows you to make something solid from an idea or a less-concrete idea. We created moulds using wooden blocks, clay and potatoes.

I used what I'd learned in Jesmonite / Fabrication Tech studies to make my outcome for 1.1.

With some help, I built a display case from perspex, perspex tubes, metal rods and bolts. I used the line bender to create little perspex display numbers that stand up by themselves. I also used wet on wet sanding paper to make the edges of everything super smooth. Creating a nice finish on things is really satisfying, and super important, in my opinion, when making something for display.

Metals

I enjoyed using the spot welder to fuse pieces of steel together. I also designed a fun way to get the curves in the rods by clamping two bolts in the vice and pulling it around them. I spaced the bolts close together to get small zig-zags and further apart to get larger zig-zags.

Bending an aluminium sheet like this was not something I thought was possible. Creating texture in a piece of metal like this opened my eyes to the material's potential. I can see myself using this technique if I ever want to create an architectural model. The big steel wire frames helped me to visualise scale for model making in 1.4.

Plastics

It's lovely to feel you have a partial understanding of a material you spend so much of your time surrounded by. I'm fascinated by the categorisation of these plastics, and how it's pot luck if something you find can be recycled- all depending on what number is in its 'little triangle' (recycling symbol) and therefore what kind of plastic is. I particularly enjoyed working with HDPE (High Density Polyethylene). I acquired six milk bottles, chopped them up, and melted them into a lovely HDPE slab with green and blue flecks in it from the milk bottle lids. I plan to save this piece of plastic for the future, when I find something that I would like to make out of it.

Bioplastics (below) are really fascinating, because you can make them from things you have in the kitchen cupboard (milk/gelatine) and in that sense they are also completely renewable and biodegradable. I loved all of the textures and colours and transparencies.

Melting of plastics was something as a group we seemed to get really engaged with. In 1.4, a few group members melted down some coffee cup lids we found at Vauxhall Bus Station and made little model people.

Interesting textures could be achieved by leaving plastics in the oven for too long (although this was not encouraged). I found a piece that was already overheated and had bubbles in it and slump moulded it into a square bowl shape.

Methods and Processes (Working Document) 08/01/18-Present

Methods and Processes is a Module on the Design Course that allows us to build skills in order to be better Studio Practitioners, and ultimately aid us in our production of work. We did workshops on model making/thinking through cardboard, ideation, research and one called 'speculate, substantiate' which I enjoyed the most (I think), but am yet to decipher the intentions of. There are still four more workshops to complete in the series of eight.

Thinking through Cardboard/Model making was fun, because it allowed us to generate 3D outcomes and test our ideas relatively quickly. I found value in the cardboard models being something that could be easily re-sculpted, meaning I wasn't too precious about them, and could generate ideas more freely. I suppose the real skill in this is learning to use this re-moldable quality of the cardboard up to a certain point in order to let creativity flow, but once nearing a final outcome, the models need to be more precise, intricate and neat. Knowing when this point is, however, might be challenging to find, but I am sure now I am aware of it that it will come with time and be easier to accomplish.

Ideation was a difficult day, it involved what seemed like a lot of tedious thinking. I think this is probably a standard feeling the first time a workshop like this evolves and reveals itself, becuase as a student, you aren't quite sure what is supposed to be happening, or where you are supposed to end up. The main idea, however, was that the tutor (Stuart) did not know anything about the list of topics he brought into the room, and together (50 Students +1 Tutor) would unpick the subject matter long enough to have some 'interesting ideas'. Although we were warned that they would not necessarily be 'good ideas', it was implied that if we worked hard enough that the ideas would at least be 'interesting'.

After a process of elimination, the topic of "Social TV" was chosen. It was here that the power of words and naming things in interesting ways revealed itself to me. If the topic had just been "Social" then I think we would have struggled to find something to talk about, but by pairing it with the word "TV" we were forced to think about the Social related interactions that involve TV, and the TV related interactions that involve Social. An avenue of exploration opened up, wide enough that we would have something to talk about, but narrow enough that we could dig relatively deep within the given constraints, and eventually find something meaningful and worthwhile. (This said, I remember the process, but none of the outcomes. This is good, because I now know a good process for Ideation, and can apply it to other scenarios and subject matter).

Research day felt very tedious again. We were asked to research a given building down the Old Kent Road. Some people had Chicken Shops, Barbers, Tattoo Parlours, Pubs and Book Shops. We had Deptford Town Hall. This proved a useful task in delegating different research tasks to different people. In dividing up the smaller tasks we wanted to accomplish, we were able to conquer the task as a whole. We were encouraged to find 'alternative' or 'creative' ways to research the buildings, and document this research, too. I decided to go and draw the town hall, and make some rubbings of its more interesting architectural features. Some of the better presentations came from the people who had taken more 'abstract' approaches, and formed unusual ways of undertaking and presenting their research. This aligns with the 'Design and Meaning' Lecture series, whereby it is good to be the 'purple cow'. What Sean Hall forgets to mention with this is that if everyone decides to be the '*PURPLE* cow', we will just be a room of purple cows. However, if everyone takes on an unusual form, as well as an unusual colour, we will end up with a "Rainbow Zoo" of Designers and Creators and collectively, in being as different from eachother as possible, will manage to be a very interesting group of Research Gatherers and Idea Generators.

"Speculate, Substantiate" involved forming theories without solid facts, and then providing evidence to prove/support or make truth of the theories, or at least make them semi-believable/realistic. I felt somewhat limited by the nature of the booklet we were given to carry out the process, but perhaps this was the point. I am used to the large A3 pages of my sketchbook, and not feeling restricted by space or the staple in the corner of the page. I also think that this would be a very useful tool for authors or illustrators, as it seemed very geared towards story telling. Initially it did not feel like it was geared towards Designers. It did, however, begin to slot into place when Sean Hall was talking to us about story telling, and how it is a valuable tool in designing. I'll have to look over my notes to substantiate this interpretation. I felt very silly phoning up companies in the Yellow Pages in order to answer questions about my work and the task. I did not feel like phoning them was justified, as the inconvenience it caused them was greater than the benefit of the tiny piece of information I would have been obtaining. I think I will use this method in the future, but only secondary to emails, letter writing, and in-person visits and conversations. And more importantly, only if the piece of information I want to obtain is specific and valuable enough to warrant interrupting someone's work day. I feel like as a Designer, you have to be very careful about how you approach people, as otherwise, they lose all respect for you and your work. The last thing I want to do is appear rude or obnoxious. The attitude towards this task was that we had to obtain information regardless of what the receiver or our onslaught felt; whether they were open to it or not. This does not align with my ethos or ethic as a person, let alone a designer, and I suppose this reflection has helped me to understand this.

Reflection on Illustration, 24/12/17

I was told not to illustrate. Avoid Illustrating things.

This hurt my feelings, because I enjoy illustrating. It helps me think. When I'm struggling for ideas it helps me put them down on paper and visualise them. Perhaps this "not illustrating" should have been pitched as "don't use illustration as a final outcome" and instead illustration should be used as my in-betweens, and if something good comes from it, great, but don't stop there.

Having this conflict though, forced me to question things, and although I didn't have an opinion on this particular topic before someone expressed their own opinion I learned that mine conflicted with theirs. I learned that I disagreed with them, and in that, over the past week or so since it was said, I have formed my own opinion and come to a realisation of how, in this case illustration, works for me.

Starting Points 14/12/17

I suppose the biggest problem is where to start with this. Many suggestions have been made for going on walks, and I agree, a great deal can be observed, collected and thought about on walks. Going to places that one has not been before often sheds light on things in the back of one's mind, or forces a different angle of thinking. Going on an 'aimless' walk, where direction, distance and end goal are not the primary concern allows decision making to be in-the-moment. An immediate engagement and response to and with one's surroundings.

Personally, I often prefer ambling in a round-about direction of where I need to go. I once walked from King's Cross Station to the Victoria Miro Gallery (For Yayoi Kusama's Infinity Mirrors). I decided for whatever reason not to use Public Transport, and simply knew that I was on the correct road and kept walking. I found a number of treasures that I would not have had access to on other occasions, and on the whole was very grateful for the exposure. Not to mention the sense of clarity I experienced when arriving at the gallery, and having my eyes attuned for observation by this point.

I would like to embark on a new project, that involves tracing my Family History. I've been exceedingly lucky in having inherited the Family Bible that dates back to 1868. This will be a fabulous research project, and I'm thinking that as a sideline I could potentially look for something to collect. It's just about keeping the thought in the back of my mind.

Rarity and Value 13/12/17

I've been thinking about chance, rarity, value and how these things interplay and play a vital role in creating novelty. There are games that place particular emphasis on certain things in order to make them valuable, perhaps what is most fascinating is the fact that these entities are only valued in these artificial environments. Like the Yellow Car Game for example, in which yellow cars are valuable as spotting one allows you to punch the other players. In tutorial we talked about collections of these by-chance things; for example a collection of books that were released and then recalled due to errors, coins collected purely for their sounds, or abandoned items of clothing in parks (Lost Winter Garments).

The challenge has been set for me to create my own collection of rarities, the only problem is I do not know what this is or how to set about finding it. I suppose the only way to discover this is to go out and start taking photos...

For example this miscellaneous shopping trolley left on the pavement. I wonder how many other shopping trolleys have strayed far from their original homes. I suppose that by collecting enough images and making an archive, eventually this topic could become interesting.
